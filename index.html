Why a lineage matters

Every belt tied at this academy is tied to a hand that goes back to the source.

Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu isn't a brand you buy — it's a chain of teaching, passed person to person, correction by correction, for over a hundred years. A lineage is proof of where the knowledge came from and who is accountable for it. Here is ours, in order.

1882Tokyo, Japan
Jigoro Kano
JKYour photo

Jigoro Kano

Founder of Judo · The Source

A schoolteacher who refined the brawling jujutsu of old Japan into a system built on leverage and efficiency — maximum effect, minimum effort. In 1882 he opened the Kodokan. Every grappler in this lineage is, eventually, his student.

1917Japan → Belém, Brazil
Mitsuyo Maeda
MMYour photo

Mitsuyo Maeda

"Conde Koma" · The Carrier

Kodokan champion and prizefighter who took Kano's art around the world and put it down for good in Brazil. In Belém he taught a teenager named Carlos Gracie — the moment the line crossed the ocean.

1925Rio de Janeiro, Brazil
Carlos Gracie Sr
CGYour photo

Carlos Gracie Sr

Patriarch · The Adaptor

Maeda's student, and the man who turned Japanese jiu-jitsu into something new. With his brothers he opened the family's first academy and built the philosophy — and the family — that would carry the art for generations. Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu starts here.

1950sRio de Janeiro, Brazil
Carlson Gracie Sr
CGYour photo

Carlson Gracie Sr

The Competitor · The Coach

Carlos's eldest son, and the fiercest fighter the family ever produced. He defended the Gracie name in the ring for years, then became its greatest teacher — building a team that ruled Vale Tudo and turned out champions by the dozen.

The fork

The Gracie tree splits here. Our line follows Carlson — the open-competition, team-first branch that put jiu-jitsu to the test against all comers — not the self-defense house of his uncle Hélio. We come from the side that fought.

1996Rio → Los Angeles, USA
Rodrigo Medeiros

Rodrigo Medeiros

6th Degree · Carlson Gracie Black Belt

Promoted to black belt by Carlson Gracie himself — five-time Brazilian champion, multi-time Pan-American champion. In 1996 he moved to the U.S. alongside his master and founded the BJJ Revolution Team. The line lands in California.

John Veloso Ouano

5th Degree · Founder, Team Tsunami

Carlson Gracie's first U.S. student in 1995 — and the first black belt Rodrigo Medeiros ever promoted. A co-founder of the BJJ Revolution Team, he launched Tsunami Brazilian Jiu-Jitsu in 2013, now over 20 schools across the U.S., New Zealand, and Australia. This is the professor who tied Noah's black belt.

Noah Arnold Tillis

4th Degree Black Belt under John Ouano · Founder & Head Instructor

Noah founded Tillis BJJ in Whittier in 2011, a fourth-degree black belt with more than two decades on the mats. He started competing two months into training and didn't stop for eleven years — across wrestling, gi, no-gi, kickboxing, and amateur MMA, turning pro at eighteen. They called him the Submission Magician, a finish-first style he still teaches. He began instructing at sixteen, and that's what his school is now built on: world champions, pro fighters, and first-week beginners training side by side.
